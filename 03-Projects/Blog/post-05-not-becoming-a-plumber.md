## Threads

### 1. 30-Year US Treasury Yield at 5.19%
Highest level since 2007. Investors selling US bonds → prices down, yields up → they demand higher returns to hold long-term US debt. Mechanism: elevated inflation expectations make fixed bond payments worth less in real terms, so yields need to rise to attract buyers.

Personal framing: structural inflation drivers (wars, energy crises, government deficits) vs. deflationary forces of AI (everything physical/energy-intensive gets expensive; everything digital/knowledge-based gets cheap). Raises questions about UBI resurfacing as a political idea. Reference point: "Ready Player One" (Spielberg) — people living in resource-scarce physical reality escaping into the OASIS. Not a direct analogy but the structural split feels real.

### 2. Y Combinator RFS Summer 2026 — AI-Native Companies
YC's request for startups flagged two particularly compelling categories:
1. **Software for Agents**: tooling built for AI agents as the primary user, not humans
2. **Company Brain**: consolidating a company's scattered institutional knowledge (hows, whys, processes) into structured data that AI can actually manage — essentially the knowledge infrastructure layer

Thesis: the first wave of "GPT wrappers" is giving way to demand for genuinely AI-native companies and companies building on top of (or beneath) the AI infrastructure layer that has developed over the last year. "Company Brain" is interesting because it's the kind of thing that sounds boring until you realize it's foundational.

### 3. Paul Graham on Taste + Taste-Washing
PG tweet (Feb 14, 2026): "In the AI age, taste will become even more important. When anyone can make anything, the big differentiator is what you choose to make."

Response: taste alone isn't the moat — taste comes from caring deeply about something specific. The harder-to-replicate quality is empathy. In music (where publishing a song on Spotify now costs less than a subscription), technically impressive songs don't spread — the things that feel understood do. You can't prompt your way to that.

The irony: the companies building this technology are doing exactly the opposite. This is what the New Yorker calls "taste-washing" — appropriating the aesthetic of human craft and slowness to make the world forget that your product threatens the livelihoods of the people who create those things. Examples:
- Anthropic opening a café
- Palantir (AI-powered kill chain software) releasing a French chore coat workwear line

PG is probably right that taste will be the moat. Just not sure it belongs to who he thinks it does.
