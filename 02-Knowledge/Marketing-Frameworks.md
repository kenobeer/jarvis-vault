# Marketing Frameworks

**What I learned:**
A set of core marketing concepts worth knowing — collected as a reference during early content/marketing thinking. Covers behavioral economics applied to marketing (mental accounting, social proof), content strategy (pillars, snackable content, CTAs), and performance measurement (KPIs). Mental accounting is the key insight: people value money differently depending on context (e.g. paying at the pump vs. at a bar feels different even if the cost is the same).

**My take:**
These are building blocks, not a strategy. The interesting ones are mental accounting and social proof — both rooted in how humans actually make decisions rather than how rational models say they should. Content pillars and KPIs are more mechanical but necessary for any distribution effort.

**Open questions:**

## Concepts

**Mental accounting** — people assign different psychological value to the same amount of money depending on context. Classic example: petrol station vs. bar — the same €2 feels cheap at one and expensive at the other. Implication: frame pricing and value relative to the right reference point.

**Social proof** — more followers / subscribers signals credibility, which attracts more followers. Bootstrapping this is the hard part; early growth is disproportionately harder than later growth.

**Content pillars** — the 3–5 recurring themes that define what a channel is about. Gives audience a reason to stay and makes content planning easier.

**CTA (call to action)** — every piece of content should have one clear next action for the reader/viewer. Often missing or too vague.

**Snackable content** — short, digestible, self-contained. Works for top-of-funnel. Risk: sacrifices depth and authority.

**KPIs** — define them before you need them. For newsletters: open rate, CTR, subscriber growth, unsubscribe rate, referral rate.

**Emotional trigger / branding** — people act on emotion, justify with logic. Brand is what makes them feel something before they've read the content.
