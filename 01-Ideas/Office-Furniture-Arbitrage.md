# Office Furniture Arbitrage — Instant Liquidation Broker

## Concept in one sentence
Act as a free liquidation partner for downsizing European companies: clear premium office furniture (Herman Miller, Vitra) within 48 hours at zero cost to the seller, then resell to Series A startups at a 50% discount, capturing the spread.

---

## The core asymmetry (why this works in theory)
Corporate tenants face real "make good" penalties from landlords if furniture is left behind. Giving it away genuinely saves them money compared to their alternatives (disposal fees, penalty clauses, or paid movers). The startup buyer trades cash for a premium good at half price. Both sides win on their primary constraint — seller on time/penalty avoidance, buyer on cost. This is a textbook arbitrage structure and the logic is sound.

---

## Objective assessment

### What is genuinely strong
- **The pain is real and verifiable.** Make-good clauses are standard in commercial leases. This is not an assumed problem — it can be confirmed with one conversation with any commercial real estate lawyer.
- **Asset quality holds value.** Herman Miller and Vitra chairs retail at EUR 1,000-2,000+ new. At a 50% haircut they are still expensive goods. Demand from startups as a hiring perk is plausible and the ESG / circular economy framing gives a modern sales hook.
- **The asset-light broker model is a real improvement over the warehouse version.** Eliminating storage removes the single largest structural cost and transforms this from a capital-heavy arbitrage play into a margin-efficient matching business.
- **Bootstrappable.** No VC needed. First deal can be funded from petty cash if logistics are pre-negotiated. This is the right funding path for this type of business.

### What is genuinely weak

**Operational complexity of the 48-hour window is severely underestimated.**
Pre-selling inventory that does not yet exist (still sitting in someone's office) to buyers who must commit before seeing it requires an extremely warm, trust-based buyer network built before deal one. In practice: photograph everything, catalog by SKU and condition, blast a curated buyer list, receive commitments, coordinate pickup logistics — all within roughly two working days while coordinating with a facilities manager who has fifty other things to do. This is not simple operations. It is a high-tempo logistics sprint that fails if any single link breaks.

**The broker model has a structural loyalty problem.**
A broker without inventory is an introducer. There is nothing stopping the startup from going directly to the facilities manager on the second deal. The value added is discovery on deal one. After that, the relationship lives between the two principals. Repeat revenue depends entirely on continuous deal sourcing, not on a sticky product.

**Revenue is lumpy and unpredictable by design.**
Each liquidation is a one-off event triggered by someone else's lease expiration. Deal flow cannot be reliably forecast, compressed, or manufactured. This is a lifestyle-business cash-flow pattern, not a compounding one.

**Margin is squeezed from both sides.**
The seller does not care what resale price you achieve — they just want the furniture gone. This means you negotiate price entirely with the buyer, who knows they are buying distressed inventory. Real margin may be lower than the headline unit economics suggest, especially once labor (specialist movers in high-rises are expensive) and transport are fully accounted for.

**No structural moat.**
The competitive advantage is a network of facilities managers and startup ops leads. This is pure relationship capital — valuable, but not defensible at scale. A well-resourced competitor can replicate the network in 12-18 months. There is no technology, data network effect, or regulatory barrier.

**Deal flow is geographic and hard to scale.**
You must be physically present in the city where the liquidation happens. Expansion to Munich or Zurich means building a new local network from scratch each time. Growth is linear, not exponential.

**Competition exists and was not addressed.**
Commercial furniture liquidation is an established category in DACH (Büromöbel Second Hand resellers, specialist auctioneers, asset recovery firms). The differentiation pitch — speed, startup-friendly branding, ESG framing — is not trivial, but it is not unique. A first-principles assessment of why incumbents cannot copy this pitch is missing from the thesis.

**The "Series A startup" buyer may be too slow.**
Startups at Series A have procurement processes. Getting a purchase approved for used furniture in 48 hours may require escalating to a CFO. Individual buyers (Willhaben, eBay Kleinanzeigen) would move faster and at no worse a price for commodity items. The startup channel is more defensible as a brand story but may not be the fastest path to clearing inventory.

## Honest unit economics (back of envelope)

Assume a 100-person office being cleared:
- 100 Aeron chairs (retail EUR 1,800) sold at 50% = EUR 90,000 gross revenue
- 100 sit-stand desks (retail EUR 1,200) sold at 50% = EUR 60,000 gross revenue
- Total gross: EUR 150,000 per deal
- Less: specialist movers for a high-rise (estimate EUR 10,000-20,000 per day, 2 days minimum), freight coordination, insurance, time cost
- Realistic net per large deal: EUR 80,000-120,000 if buyer is pre-lined up and logistics are tight
- At 10 deals/year: EUR 800K-1.2M revenue. At 15 deals/year: EUR 1.2M-1.8M.

These numbers look attractive, but 10-15 large office liquidations per year in one city requires a significant pipeline of CRE and facilities contacts. This is a full-time sales and operations job for at least two people.

---

## Verdict (as of April 2026)

**The arbitrage thesis is real.** The core logic works. If you can solve the pre-sell problem (warm buyer network built before signing your first liquidation contract) and the logistics problem (reliable specialist movers on short notice), this is a legitimate EUR 1M-3M/year cash-flow business for a small team.

**The ceiling is the problem.** This business does not scale beyond 2-3 cities without building parallel local operations in each market. It will not become a platform. It will not attract institutional capital. For someone targeting VC or a high-growth founding path, this is a distraction unless run as a deliberate short-term cash-generation vehicle (2-3 years, EUR 500K-1M extracted, then exit or wind down).

**The pivot to broker/direct-ship is correct** but only moves the hardest problem (buyer acquisition) to the front of the process rather than eliminating it.

**If pursued, the right first step is not finding a liquidation.** It is building a list of 30 startup ops leads who will commit to buying premium furniture on short notice, and getting soft pre-commitments from 3-5 of them before touching a single desk.
